The Imperfect Storm in Our Area

A calm, factual, community-led campaign for independent testing, transparency, and protection for local families.

Our area faces a combination of environmental and planning risks that have never been assessed together. Each issue alone is serious — but together they form an Imperfect Storm. Our goal is simple: independent testing, openness, and protection for the people who live here.

What's Happening in Our Area

Five serious issues, never assessed together

The Ardley incinerator, PFAS contamination, sludge and slurry storage, daily HGV traffic through Heyford Park, and housing built on or near contaminated land all intersect in our community. No authority has ever looked at these risks as a whole. Here is each part of the storm.

01 — INFRASTRUCTURE

Ardley Incinerator

  • Originally refused locally, then approved following ministerial intervention.
  • Burns more than 300,000 tonnes of waste each year.
  • Produces emissions, fine particulates, and heavy traffic.
  • No cumulative health assessment has been carried out for the housing nearby.
02 — WATER & CHEMICALS

PFAS in Gallos Brook

  • PFAS reported in watercourses linked to the former RAF Upper Heyford site.
  • These "forever chemicals" are persistent, bioaccumulative, and raise long-term health questions.
  • No PFAS-aware assessment has been made for residents, children, or wildlife.
03 — LAND & RUNOFF

Sludge & Slurry Fields

  • Large mounds stored on farmland for months at a time.
  • May contain PFAS, pharmaceuticals, microplastics, and heavy metals.
  • Carries a runoff risk into local ditches and streams.
  • A potential hydrological link to Gallos Brook.
04 — PLANNING

Housing on Contaminated Land

  • Thousands of homes built within roughly a mile of the incinerator.
  • Many on or near historic Ministry of Defence contamination.
  • No cumulative modelling for the families now living here.
05 — TRAFFIC & AIR

HGVs Through Heyford Park

Heyford Park experiences 300–400 heavy goods vehicles every single day using Camp Road as the main access route through the middle of the estate — the same road lined with homes, the school, playgrounds and shops. These lorries release significant daily pollution directly inside the residential area:

540–720 kg
of CO₂ every day
2.4–3.2 kg
of NOₓ every day — linked to asthma, heart and lung disease
30–40 g
of PM₂.₅ every day — fine particles that penetrate deep into the lungs
  • This level of pollution is normally associated with major roads — not a so-called "family-friendly" housing development.
  • Children walking to school, families opening windows, and residents using gardens are exposed to continuous, cumulative emissions.
  • The estate has been designed around an HGV corridor, with no bypass and no separation between industrial traffic and residential life.
  • The result is a high-pollution environment being built and marketed as a healthy community.

Who Controls the Land

Corporate Structure & Accountability at Heyford Park

Heyford Park is controlled by a small group of private individuals through a network of interlinked companies, many of which share almost identical names. Each company typically owns a different slice of land or phase of development — a structure that is difficult for residents to understand and even harder to scrutinise.

DIRECTORS & BOARDS

A Small Group, Dozens of Companies

  • The core directors and shareholders are Ian Amdur, Gary Silver, and Paul Silver.
  • They sit on dozens of Heyford-related company boards.
  • Many of the companies share almost identical names, making them difficult for residents to tell apart and scrutinise.
FINANCE

Outstanding Bank Charges

  • Numerous outstanding bank charges are registered against these companies at Companies House.
  • The charges show different parts of the former airbase have been repeatedly used as security for loans and refinancing arrangements over many years.
  • This is a common model in large-scale development — but the sheer number of entities and charges raises questions about transparency, financial risk, and long-term accountability.
THE ORIGINAL SALE

An Unresolved Question

  • The land was first owned by the North Oxfordshire Consortium, a partnership of some of the UK's largest national housebuilders.
  • Instead of developing the site themselves, they sold it to the much smaller Dorchester Group.
  • No clear public explanation has ever been given for why major national developers stepped away from such a large and potentially profitable site.
  • It is also unclear what due diligence was carried out before the sale.

Taken together — the complex company structure, the multiple charges, and the lack of clarity around the original sale — residents are left with important unanswered questions about who holds responsibility for the land, the financing, and the long-term environmental and community impacts of development at Heyford Park.

Company, directorship and charge information is drawn from public records held at Companies House. This page asks questions about transparency and accountability; it does not allege wrongdoing by any individual or company.
